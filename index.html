Introduction

This project analyzes the fictional speech of Agatha Christie's two famous detectives — Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple.

The main question I explore is:

How do their speech styles differ, and what do these differences reveal about their characters and roles in the narrative?

I created a custom dataset using direct quotes from Christie’s novels and short stories where each character is the lead detective. Using Python in Google Colab, I analyzed lexical frequency, sentence length, part-of-speech distribution, and discourse markers (like “dear,” “my friend,” “I think,” “perhaps”). I also used named entity recognition and syntactic parsing to study pronoun use and reference patterns.

The project combines quantitative linguistic analysis with literary insights. It shows how Christie uses language to reflect each character’s personality, background, and method of investigation — making them distinct and memorable.

Literature Review

This project investigates how character is constructed through dialogue, combining computational stylistics with literary analysis. While Poirot’s and Marple’s speech styles are often remarked on, they lack thorough data-driven study.

Key authors and contributions:

  • Toolan (2009): Shows how linguistic features like modality and deixis build narrative identity.
  • Burrows (2002): Uses word frequency to differentiate fictional voices, revealing subtle character traits.
  • Mahlberg (2013) & Culpeper (2001): Demonstrate how character speech patterns reflect social identity in corpus stylistics.
  • Makinen (2006) & Rowland (2010): Analyze Christie’s narrative and gender politics, but pay less attention to stylistic differences between her detectives.
  • Leech and Short (1981): Present dialogue as a key marker of character, not just plot.
  • Haley (2012): Argues textual evidence should be seen as socially coded performance, not just transparent communication.

Conclusion:
Building on these works, this study applies computational tools to analyze Poirot’s formal, foreign-influenced style and Marple’s informal, hedging speech. It highlights how Christie’s language choices shape distinct and culturally embedded detective identities.

Method Comparison

Corpus Preparation

Named entity recognition and speaker tagging isolate Poirot’s and Marple’s direct speech for focused analysis.

Lexical & Syntactic Analysis

Frequency charts and syntactic parsing reveal differences—Poirot’s formal, logical style versus Marple’s conversational tone.

Pragmatic Markers

Co-occurrence networks show how discourse markers like hedges and politeness features vary and evolve for each character.

Temporal Trends

Line plots track stylistic changes over time, such as the decline of Poirot’s French phrases and the rise of Marple’s modal expressions.

Each method builds on the others to offer a clear, data-driven portrait of Christie’s iconic detectives through their speech.
