Literature Review

This project investigates how character is constructed through dialogue, combining computational stylistics with literary analysis. While Poirot‚Äôs and Marple‚Äôs speech styles are often remarked on, they lack thorough data-driven study.

Key authors and contributions:

  • Toolan (2009): Shows how linguistic features like modality and deixis build narrative identity.
  • Burrows (2002): Uses word frequency to differentiate fictional voices, revealing subtle character traits.
  • Mahlberg (2013) & Culpeper (2001): Demonstrate how character speech patterns reflect social identity in corpus stylistics.
  • Makinen (2006) & Rowland (2010): Analyze Christie‚Äôs narrative and gender politics, but pay less attention to stylistic differences between her detectives.
  • Leech and Short (1981): Present dialogue as a key marker of character, not just plot.
  • Haley (2012): Argues textual evidence should be seen as socially coded performance, not just transparent communication.

Conclusion:
Building on these works, this study applies computational tools to analyze Poirot‚Äôs formal, foreign-influenced style and Marple‚Äôs informal, hedging speech. It highlights how Christie‚Äôs language choices shape distinct and culturally embedded detective identities.

Data Preparation

üìö Data Preparation

This project uses publicly available full-text transcriptions of Agatha Christie‚Äôs short story collections: Hercule Poirot: The Complete Short Stories and Miss Marple: The Complete Short Stories, sourced from the Internet Archive.

üß† Quote Extraction

Since the focus is on the speech styles of Poirot and Marple, only their spoken lines were extracted. This was done with a custom Python script in Google Colab, using two steps:

  1. Extracting all text within quotation marks (assumed to be character speech) via regular expressions.
  2. Filtering quotes by checking if the character‚Äôs name ("poirot" or "marple") appears nearby, to exclude other characters‚Äô speech.

The resulting quotes were saved into separate files for each character, forming the raw dataset. No further preprocessing (tokenization, lemmatization) was done at this stage.

Final dataset: 3,894 quotes from Poirot and 670 from Marple. Although unbalanced, both provide enough material for stylistic analysis. All narrative and non-verbal content was excluded to focus purely on dialogue.

Limitations include possible omission of indirect or subtly attributed speech due to the filtering method. However, this approach is reproducible and scalable for extracting targeted dialogue from large literary corpora.
