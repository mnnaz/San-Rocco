Introduction

This project analyzes the fictional speech of Agatha Christie's two famous detectives — Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple.

The main question I explore is:

How do their speech styles differ, and what do these differences reveal about their characters and roles in the narrative?

I created a custom dataset using direct quotes from Christie’s novels and short stories where each character is the lead detective. Using Python in Google Colab, I analyzed lexical frequency, sentence length, part-of-speech distribution, and discourse markers (like “dear,” “my friend,” “I think,” “perhaps”). I also used named entity recognition and syntactic parsing to study pronoun use and reference patterns.

The project combines quantitative linguistic analysis with literary insights. It shows how Christie uses language to reflect each character’s personality, background, and method of investigation — making them distinct and memorable.

Literature Review

This project investigates how character is constructed through dialogue, combining computational stylistics with literary analysis. While Poirot’s and Marple’s speech styles are often remarked on, they lack thorough data-driven study.

Key authors and contributions:

  • Toolan (2009): Shows how linguistic features like modality and deixis build narrative identity.
  • Burrows (2002): Uses word frequency to differentiate fictional voices, revealing subtle character traits.
  • Mahlberg (2013) & Culpeper (2001): Demonstrate how character speech patterns reflect social identity in corpus stylistics.
  • Makinen (2006) & Rowland (2010): Analyze Christie’s narrative and gender politics, but pay less attention to stylistic differences between her detectives.
  • Leech and Short (1981): Present dialogue as a key marker of character, not just plot.
  • Haley (2012): Argues textual evidence should be seen as socially coded performance, not just transparent communication.

Conclusion:
Building on these works, this study applies computational tools to analyze Poirot’s formal, foreign-influenced style and Marple’s informal, hedging speech. It highlights how Christie’s language choices shape distinct and culturally embedded detective identities.

Data Extraction

Since the focus is on the speech styles of Poirot and Marple, only their spoken lines were extracted. This was done with a custom Python script in Google Colab, using two steps:

  • Extracting all text within quotation marks (assumed to be character speech) via regular expressions.
  • Filtering quotes by checking if the character’s name ("poirot" or "marple") appears nearby, to exclude other characters’ speech.

The resulting quotes were saved into separate files for each character, forming the raw dataset. No further preprocessing (tokenization, lemmatization) was done at this stage.

The final dataset contains 3,894 quotes from Poirot and 670 from Marple. Although unbalanced, both provide enough material for stylistic analysis. All narrative and non-verbal content was excluded to focus purely on dialogue.

Corpus Compilation and Preprocessing

Two corpora were created from full quotes and short stories featuring Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple. Using named entity recognition (NER) and custom speaker tagging, direct speech from each character was extracted, producing character-specific dialogue datasets.

Preprocessing steps included:

  • Reading raw text files
  • Converting text to lowercase
  • Tokenizing into words
  • Removing stop words (e.g., “and,” “the”)
  • Filtering out non-alphabetic tokens (punctuation, numbers)

Frequency distributions were calculated for each corpus, identifying the top 100 most frequent words combined for comparative analysis.

Lexical and Syntactic Feature Analysis

Poirot’s speech is formal and analytical, while Marple’s is informal and conversational. Top word frequency charts highlight these differences. Syntactic analysis shows Poirot uses structured, declarative sentences; Marple uses more subordinate, conversational forms. PCA clustering supports these contrasts.

Pragmatic Marker Trends

A heatmap shows how Poirot’s use of pragmatic markers changes over time. Hedges and intensifiers are more common in early texts, while politeness and indirect forms decline.

Emphatic expressions increase, and fillers stay consistently low. These shifts reflect both his precision and evolving narrative role.

Sentiment Analysis Summary

Sentiment polarity scores (from -1 to +1) were assigned to each sentence of dialogue. Poirot’s speech shows a wider emotional range and leans slightly positive, reflecting his expressive style.

Marple’s tone is more neutral and balanced, aligning with her calm, observant nature. These differences support their distinct character roles.

Discussion

This study shows clear differences in how Poirot and Marple use pragmatic markers. Poirot’s speech is strategic and theatrical, with clustered markers like well and actually supporting his logical, performative style. Marple’s speech is more diffuse and reflective, using markers like I suppose and you see to soften and frame her observations.

These patterns align with their literary personas: Poirot is deliberate and authoritative, Marple is modest and conversational. Although the analysis focuses on marker co-occurrence without deeper context, the network approach effectively reveals how speech shapes character identity and narrative roles.

Conclusion

This study used computational stylistics to analyze the distinct speech styles of Agatha Christie’s detectives, Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple. Poirot’s dialogue is formal and theatrical, featuring French interjections and pragmatic markers like “well” and “actually,” reflecting his meticulous and intellectual persona. Miss Marple’s speech is more conversational and hedged, using phrases like “I suppose” and “maybe,” highlighting her observational and intuitive nature.

The linguistic differences underscore their unique narrative roles and character identities. Over time, Poirot’s language shows a decline in French expressions, while Marple’s style remains consistent. Despite some limitations, the multi-method approach effectively reveals how dialogue shapes character identity and narrative function. Future work could deepen these insights with more semantic and prosodic analysis.

Overall, Poirot and Marple’s speech styles are integral to their portrayal, illustrating how language constructs distinct personalities within detective fiction.
